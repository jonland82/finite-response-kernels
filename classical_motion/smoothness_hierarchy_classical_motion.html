On the Smoothness Hierarchy of Time Differentiation in Classical Motion

J. Landers Brief expository exploration

1. Context and Claim

Classical mechanics begins with a path \(x : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}^n\). Differentiate once and one sees velocity; differentiate twice and one sees acceleration; differentiate again and the sharpness of the model starts to show. Textbook forces are often allowed to switch on instantaneously, which means acceleration is allowed to jump.

This short exploration follows that jump one derivative upward. An instantaneous change is not just a dramatic onset in the lower-order variable; it is a singular object in the next derivative. The hierarchy

\[ x,\ \dot x,\ \ddot x,\ \dddot x,\ \ldots \]

is therefore more than notation. It is a ladder of regularity: assumptions made higher up constrain what can happen below. In distributional language, an instantaneous onset of acceleration appears as

\[ \dddot x(t)=\Delta a\,\delta(t-t_0), \]

where \(\Delta a\) is the jump in acceleration at time \(t_0\).

Writing the onset this way is not an insistence that nature contains infinite spikes. It is a way to keep honest books: ideal discontinuities are shorthand for unresolved mediation. Ballistic mediation concentrates influence on sharp arrival-time surfaces; diffusive mediation aggregates many small delays into smooth kernels. Both recover \(\delta\)-like behavior only as limiting cases.

The central claim is:

Instantaneous onset of acceleration is equivalent to the presence of a distributional impulse in jerk.
Conversely, excluding impulsive jerk forbids discontinuous acceleration.

The result is a small but useful lens: smoothness assumptions are not just mathematical tidiness. They encode locality, causality, and finite response.

2. Mathematical Formulation and Interpretation

Let \(x(t)\) denote the position of a particle of mass \(m>0\), and consider Newton’s second law

\[ m\ddot x(t)=F(t), \]

interpreted in the sense of distributions. Let \(H(t)\) denote the Heaviside step function and \(\delta(t)=\dot H(t)\) the Dirac delta distribution.

In practice, these distributions appear as limits of causal response kernels. Wave propagation gives a retarded response supported on a wavefront, a \(\delta\) at the arrival time. Diffusion gives Gaussian profiles. The same ideal impulse sits at the boundary of both stories.

If a constant force is idealized as switching on instantaneously,

\[ F(t)=F_0 H(t), \]

then

\[ a(t)=\ddot x(t)=\frac{F_0}{m}H(t), \] \[ j(t)=\dddot x(t)=\frac{F_0}{m}\delta(t). \]

Thus, the jump in acceleration at \(t=0\) is precisely encoded as an impulsive jerk. More generally, if \(a(t)\) has a jump discontinuity of magnitude \(\Delta a\) at time \(t_0\), then its distributional derivative contains the term \(\Delta a\,\delta(t-t_0)\). Discontinuous acceleration and impulsive jerk are therefore mathematically equivalent statements.

Imposing regularity at the level of jerk eliminates this singularity. If \(j=\dot a\) is locally integrable (or bounded), then \(a\) is absolutely continuous and cannot jump:

\[ a(t)=a(t_0)+\int_{t_0}^t j(s)\,ds. \]

The same implication propagates upward: finite snap forbids jumps in jerk, and so on.

A physically meaningful regularization arises by modeling force as the output of a causal dynamical system. For example, let

\[ \tau \dot F(t)+F(t)=F_0 H(t), \]

with response time \(\tau>0\). Then

\[ a(t)=\frac{F_0}{m}\bigl(1-e^{-t/\tau}\bigr)H(t), \] \[ j(t)=\frac{F_0}{m\tau}e^{-t/\tau}H(t), \]

which are finite and smooth for all \(t>0\). The impulsive-jerk model is recovered only in the singular limit \(\tau\to 0\), where \(j\) converges to \((F_0/m)\delta(t)\) in the distributional sense.

This formulation makes the bookkeeping explicit: instantaneous acceleration is not a primitive physical feature, but a singular limit in which the hierarchy of time differentiation has been truncated.

3. Geometry of Mediation: Ballistic and Diffusive Limits

The hierarchy becomes more intuitive when the onset is treated as the trace of a mediator with a transport geometry. In the ideal limits below, the kernel shape is not chosen for convenience; it is forced by the propagation law and symmetry.

3.1 Ballistic mediation (wave propagation) and a Dirac arrival time

Consider an idealized scalar signal \(\phi(x,t)\) propagating at speed \(c\) in \(\mathbb{R}^3\), driven by a point impulse at the origin. The retarded Green’s function \(G\) satisfies

\[ (\partial_t^2 - c^2\nabla^2)\,G(x,t)=\delta(t)\,\delta^{(3)}(x), \] \[ G(x,t)=0\ \text{for}\ t<0. \]

By finite propagation speed, influence from the origin can reach radius \(r=\|x\|\) only when the wavefront arrives, \(t=r/c\). Spherical symmetry and conservation of flux through spheres force the \(1/r\) scaling. Thus the retarded response is supported on the arrival-time surface and takes the form

\[ G_{\mathrm{ret}}(r,t)=\frac{1}{4\pi r}\,\delta\!\left(t-\frac{r}{c}\right)H(t). \]

In this ballistic limit the \(\delta\) is geometric. It records a sharp wavefront carrying the impulse to a fixed arrival time. Bandwidth, dispersion, and finite source rise time smooth the spike; the delta is what remains when those scales are sent to zero.

3.2 Diffusive mediation (many scatterings) and a Gaussian kernel

Now assume transport occurs through many small, independent scatterings. In one dimension, let \(X_N=\sum_{k=1}^N\xi_k\) with \(\xi_k\in\{+\ell,-\ell\}\) equally likely, representing a random walk with step length \(\ell\). Its characteristic function is

\[ \varphi_{X_N}(k)= \] \[ \bigl(\mathbb{E}\,e^{ik\xi_1}\bigr)^N=\bigl(\cos(k\ell)\bigr)^N. \]

For small \(\ell\) and large \(N\) with \(N\ell^2\) fixed, \(\cos(k\ell)\approx 1-\tfrac12 k^2\ell^2\), hence

\[ \bigl(\cos(k\ell)\bigr)^N \approx \] \[ \left(1-\tfrac12 k^2\ell^2\right)^N \longrightarrow \exp\!\left(-\tfrac12 k^2 N\ell^2\right), \]

which is the characteristic function of a Gaussian. Writing \(t=N\Delta t\) and \(D=\ell^2/(2\Delta t)\), the limiting density is the heat kernel

\[ p(x,t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi Dt}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{4Dt}\right). \]

Thus, in diffusive regimes the transport geometry selects a Gaussian shape. In time-domain switching, analogous profiles appear when an onset aggregates many small independent micro-delays. The distributional \(\delta\) returns only as a fast-transition limit.

4. Conclusion

The hierarchy \(x,\dot x,\ddot x,\dddot x,\ldots\) is a regularity ladder. Jumps at one level become impulsive terms one derivative higher, and excluding those impulses is exactly a smoothness assumption. The \(\delta\) terms in idealized instantaneous models are not strange objects hiding in nature; they are compact encodings of mediation occurring below the scale being resolved.

The useful punchline is that different mediation geometries select different kernel families before the singular limit is taken. Ballistic propagation concentrates influence on arrival surfaces; diffusive propagation spreads it into Gaussian kernels. Acceleration turning on instantly is therefore shorthand for a modeling choice about transport and scale separation, and the distributional formulation makes the choice visible.
