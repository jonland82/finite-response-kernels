\section{Why invert a takeoff curve?}

A naive recursive program exposes a tree of calls. Equal labels can appear
many times; memoization identifies them and leaves a directed acyclic graph
(DAG) of distinct subproblems. For input size $n\ge0$, let $N_n$ be the number
of calls in the naive tree and let $M_n$ be the number of distinct reachable
labels. Throughout, $\log$ denotes the natural logarithm. The logarithmic
overlap is
\[
S_n=\log\frac{N_n}{M_n},
\]
with one-step velocity
\[
v_n=S_{n+1}-S_n=u_n-w_n,
\]
where
\[
u_n=\log N_{n+1}-\log N_n,\qquad
w_n=\log M_{n+1}-\log M_n.
\]
If $N_n$ grows exponentially and
$\alpha:=\lim_{n\to\infty}u_n>0$ exists while $M_n$ is polynomial, then
$w_n\to0$ and $v_n\to\alpha$. That terminal
number says how fast redundancy eventually accumulates, but not how it gets
there. Two recurrences can have the same cruising speed while one accelerates
immediately and the other rings for a very long time.

The normalized takeoff profile and its causal difference are
\[
F_n=\frac{u_n}{\alpha},\qquad
\kappa_n=F_n-F_{n-1},\quad F_{-1}=0.
\]
Thus $F_n\to1$, and $\kappa$ records the response shape: delay, monotone
settling, alternating correction, or damped ringing. Forward analysis starts
from a recurrence and predicts $F$. Inverse takeoff starts from an observed
prefix of $F$ and asks what can be recovered.

A lag $j\ge1$ is measured in input steps. An \emph{observation horizon}
$T\ge1$ means that exactly the $T$ samples $F_0,\ldots,F_{T-1}$ are available.
Thus ``length $T$'' counts samples rather than the largest displayed index. A
\emph{forecast horizon} $h\ge1$ refers to the next $h$ samples
$F_T,\ldots,F_{T+h-1}$. When a maximum admissible lag is assumed, $D\ge1$
denotes that known bound.

The analogy is system identification, but the object is unusual. The
``plant'' is the redundant response of a recursion tree before memoization
quotients equal states. Classical Prony and matrix-pencil methods can estimate
damped exponential modes from samples \cite{prony,hua,plonka}. What is new is
the constrained source class and the distinction between response modes and
literal recursive branches.

\paragraph{Contributions}
The note makes five concrete advances. \textbf{First}, an exact difference
identity and root certificate lead to a complete characterization: a
profile's first $T$ samples reveal total branching and precisely the coefficients at
lags below $T$. \textbf{Second}, the unseen tail can contain exponentially many
aperiodic integer recurrences with the same speed, branch count, DAG-growth
class, and observed prefix; the resulting source ambiguity survives arbitrary
parameter-independent noise and any finite holdout placed before the hidden
lag returns. \textbf{Third}, the negative result has a constructive boundary:
given a lag bound $D$ and the exact samples through $F_D$, a triangular formula
recovers every coefficient. \textbf{Fourth}, a fixed-speed family proves that
aperiodicity does not bound the settling time, explaining the long echo trains
seen in the kernels. \textbf{Fifth}, 32.67 million blinded recovery trials map
where a classical spectral estimator works, where it fails, and how highly
accurate forecasts can coexist with incorrect modal structure.

\section{Finite-lag response and its modes}

Consider the finite-lag schema
\begin{equation}
N_n=1+\sum_{j\in J}a_jN_{n-j},\qquad N_q=1\ \text{for }q\le0,
\label{eq:recurrence}
\end{equation}
where $J\subset\{1,2,\ldots\}$ is finite and
$a_j\in\mathbb{Z}_{>0}$ (the positive integers) is the number of branches at
lag $j$. The symbol $q$ is only a boundary index. For a complex variable $z$,
define the recurrence polynomial
\[
Q(z)=1-\sum_{j\in J}a_jz^j.
\]
We call the schema \emph{aperiodic} when the greatest common divisor of the
active lag set $J$ is one.
When $\gcd(J)=1$ and $\sum_j a_j>1$, $Q$ has a unique smallest-modulus
positive root $\rho\in(0,1)$, and the terminal speed is
$\alpha=-\log\rho$. Non-dominant singularities $\zeta$ contribute normalized
correction modes
\[
\theta=\frac{\rho}{\zeta}.
\]
The affine $+1$ in \eqref{eq:recurrence} also contributes the singularity
$z=1$. The largest active $|\theta|<1$ controls the eventual settling rate,
while a complex leading mode produces ringing.

For a common terminal speed $\alpha=\log2$, the root is $\rho=1/2$ and the
coefficient constraint becomes
\begin{equation}
\sum_j a_j2^{-j}=1.
\label{eq:fixedspeed}
\end{equation}
This dyadic identity is useful experimentally: it generates many structurally
different recurrences while holding the first-order asymptotic invariant
exactly fixed.

An observed tail is modeled approximately as
\begin{equation}
Y_n:=F_n-1\approx\sum_{r=1}^{R}c_r\theta_r^n.
\label{eq:modal}
\end{equation}
Here $Y_n$ is the deviation from terminal takeoff, $R$ is the retained mode
count, $c_r$ is the amplitude of mode $r$, and $\theta_r$ is its normalized
pole. Complex modes and amplitudes occur in conjugate pairs because $Y_n$ is
real.
The approximation matters. Even when $N_n$ has a finite exponential
expansion, the logarithmic ratio defining $F_n$ introduces higher-order mode
products. A low-rank spectral fit is therefore a tail model, not an algebraic
identity for every finite prefix.

Figure~\ref{fig:atlas} gives a visual vocabulary for the inverse problem. The
top row is the takeoff profile; the bottom row is its causal difference, the
kernel actually carrying the transient shape. The same terminal level can be
approached smoothly, by sign alternation, through damped oscillation, or in
widely separated echoes.

\section{What a finite trace can contain}

The obstruction is more basic than numerical noise: a recursive signal cannot
reveal a branch whose lag has not returned. The following sequence makes that
idea exact and also gives its positive converse. Extend every schema by
$a_j=0$ off its finite support, and write $A=\sum_j a_j$.

\begin{lemma}[Exact difference response]\label{lem:difference}
Let $d_n=N_n-N_{n-1}$ for $n\ge1$ and $d_n=0$ for $n\le0$. Then
\begin{equation}
d_1=A,\qquad d_n=\sum_j a_jd_{n-j}\quad(n\ge2),
\label{eq:difference}
\end{equation}
and, as a formal power series,
\[
G_d(z):=\sum_{n\ge1}d_nz^n=\frac{Az}{Q(z)}.
\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The boundary convention gives $N_1=1+A$, hence $d_1=A$.
Subtracting \eqref{eq:recurrence} at $n-1$ from the equation at $n$ gives
\eqref{eq:difference}; multiplying by $z^n$ and summing gives the quotient.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Fixed-speed root certificate]\label{lem:root}
Suppose $a_j\ge0$, \eqref{eq:fixedspeed} holds, and
$\gcd\{j:a_j>0\}=1$, where $\gcd$ denotes greatest common divisor. Then
$\rho=1/2$ is the unique root of $Q$ in
$|z|\le1/2$. Consequently the terminal speed is $\alpha=\log2$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
If $Q(z)=0$ and $|z|\le1/2$, then
\[
1=\left|\sum_j a_jz^j\right|
 \le\sum_j a_j|z|^j\le\sum_j a_j2^{-j}=1.
\]
Equality forces $|z|=1/2$ and all active phases $z^j/|z|^j$ to equal one.
Their lag gcd is one, so $z=1/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[No uniform settling rate]\label{prop:slow}
For integers $L\ge2$, consider the aperiodic fixed-speed family
\[
Q_L(z)=1-(2^L-1)z^L-2z^{L+1},
\]
and define
\[
r_L:=\max_{\substack{Q_L(\zeta)=0\\ \zeta\ne1/2}}
\left|\frac{1/2}{\zeta}\right|.
\]
Then $r_L\to1$ as $L\to\infty$. Thus fixed terminal speed and aperiodicity do
not impose a uniform transient length.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Put $x=2z$. The root equation becomes
$f_L(x)+g_L(x)=0$, where $f_L=1-x^L$ and
$g_L=2^{-L}x^L(1-x)$. On a circle of radius
$C2^{-L}/L^2$ about $\omega_L=e^{2\pi i/L}$, Taylor expansion gives
$|f_L|\sim C2^{-L}/L$ and $|g_L|\sim2\pi2^{-L}/L$.
Choosing $C>2\pi$, Rouch\'e's theorem gives a root
$x_L=\omega_L+O(2^{-L}/L^2)$. Lemma~\ref{lem:root} gives $|x_L|>1$, while
$|x_L|\to1$; hence the associated normalized radius $|1/x_L|\to1$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Complete finite-prefix characterization]\label{thm:prefix}
Consider schemas with coefficient sequences $a=(a_j)$ and $b=(b_j)$ and a
common known terminal speed $\alpha$. Let
$A_a=\sum_j a_j$ and $A_b=\sum_j b_j$. For every observation horizon $T\ge1$,
their profiles agree on $F_0,\ldots,F_{T-1}$ if and only if $A_a=A_b$ and
\[
a_j=b_j\qquad(1\le j<T).
\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The prefix reconstructs
\[
N_n=\exp\!\left(\alpha\sum_{r=0}^{n-1}F_r\right),\qquad 1\le n\le T,
\]
and hence $d_1,\ldots,d_T$. Lemma~\ref{lem:difference} gives $A=d_1$ and the
triangular inversion
\begin{equation}
a_k=\frac{d_{k+1}-\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}a_jd_{k+1-j}}{d_1},
\qquad 1\le k<T.
\label{eq:triangular}
\end{equation}
Thus equality of profiles implies the stated equalities. Conversely,
\eqref{eq:difference} shows inductively that those equalities force
$d_1,\ldots,d_T$, and therefore the profile prefixes, to agree.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Exact recovery with a lag bound]\label{cor:positive}
Let the known integer $D\ge1$ be an upper bound on the largest lag, so that
$a_j=0$ for $j>D$. Then the $D+1$ noiseless samples
$F_0,\ldots,F_D$, together with $\alpha$, uniquely recover the full recurrence
by \eqref{eq:triangular}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proposition}[Exponential fixed-invariant ambiguity]\label{prop:family}
Fix an observation horizon $T$ and choose an integer base lag $L\ge T$. There
are
\[
K_L=\left\lfloor\frac{2^{L+1}-1}{3}\right\rfloor
\]
distinct aperiodic integer schemas with common speed $\log2$, total branch
count $2^{L+1}$, and at-most-linear memoized-state growth $M_n=O(n)$, all
agreeing on
$F_0,\ldots,F_{T-1}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For $1\le t<2^{L+1}/3$, set
\[
a_L=t,\quad a_{L+1}=2^{L+1}-3t,\quad a_{L+2}=2t.
\]
The coefficients are positive, sum to $2^{L+1}$, and satisfy
$4a_L+2a_{L+1}+a_{L+2}=2^{L+2}$, which is
\eqref{eq:fixedspeed}. Consecutive support gives aperiodicity, so
Lemma~\ref{lem:root} fixes the speed. Reachable labels are one-dimensional,
giving $M_n=O(n)$. Theorem~\ref{thm:prefix} gives the common prefix. Distinct
$t$ first separate at $F_L$ and define distinct $Q_t$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Noisy minimax obstruction]\label{cor:minimax}
Choose uniformly from the $K_L$ schemas in Proposition~\ref{prop:family} and
pass the common prefix through any parameter-independent noise channel, meaning
that the channel law depends on the clean prefix but not on its schema label.
Every source estimator $\widehat t$ satisfies
\[
\Pr(\widehat t=t)\le K_L^{-1},\qquad
\inf_{\widehat t}\sup_t\Pr_t(\widehat t\ne t)\ge1-K_L^{-1}.
\]
Here $t$ indexes the true schema, $\widehat t$ is its estimate, $\Pr$ is the
joint probability over the uniform source and noise, and $\Pr_t$ conditions on
schema $t$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
All $K_L$ observation laws are identical. Under the uniform prior, the
posterior remains uniform, giving the first bound; some parameter has success
at most the average, giving the minimax bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Finite holdout does not certify mechanism]\label{cor:holdout}
For arbitrary observation horizon $T$ and forecast horizon $h$, distinct
fixed-invariant schemas can agree on both the observed samples
$F_0,\ldots,F_{T-1}$ and the forecast targets
$F_T,\ldots,F_{T+h-1}$ while having different recurrence polynomials and full
modal spectra.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Use Proposition~\ref{prop:family} with $L\ge T+h$. The profiles agree through
$F_{T+h-1}$, while the distinct polynomials $Q_t$, each with constant
coefficient one, cannot have the same complete root multiset.
\end{proof}

The picture is now sharp. Without a lag bound, the ambiguity class can grow
exponentially and survives arbitrary parameter-independent noise. With a lag
bound, the $D+1$ samples through $F_D$ give a triangular decoder. Intuitively,
this is listening for an echo before sound reaches the
wall: unlimited estimator sophistication cannot recover absent information.
Figure~\ref{fig:collision} displays one such equivalence class and its eventual
separation.

\section{The blinded experiment}

The study used five fixed-speed families: immediate binary recursion; the
near-periodic slow family
$a_L=2^L-1$, $a_{L+1}=2$, where $L$ is its base lag; the exponentially
ambiguous finite-horizon family;
a two-scale
family with one local and one delayed channel; and random dyadic refinements
that replace one branch at lag $j$ by two branches at lag $j+1$. Every such
refinement preserves \eqref{eq:fixedspeed}.

For each hidden schema we computed an exact integer call-count sequence and
formed $F_n$. Let $\mathcal W$ denote the current set of $T$ observed indices
and define
\[
\operatorname{RMS}_{\mathcal W}(Y)
:=\left(T^{-1}\sum_{n\in\mathcal W}Y_n^2\right)^{1/2}.
\]
The estimator received only
\[
\widetilde Y_n=Y_n+\sigma
\max\{\operatorname{RMS}_{\mathcal W}(Y),10^{-12}\}\,\xi_n,
\qquad \xi_n\sim\mathcal N(0,1),
\]
not the coefficients or true roots. Here $\sigma\ge0$ is the relative noise
level and the $\xi_n$ are independent standard-normal draws. We tested windows
beginning at the origin and immediately after the maximum lag $D:=\max J$.
Relative noise levels were $0,10^{-8},10^{-5},10^{-3}$.

The baseline formed Hankel matrices
\[
(H_0)_{ij}=\widetilde Y_{i+j},\qquad
(H_1)_{ij}=\widetilde Y_{i+j+1},
\]
where $i,j\ge0$ range over entries whose subscripts lie in the observed
window. It selected an order from one through six by held-out prediction and
recovered poles from a matrix pencil truncated by singular-value decomposition
(SVD). It then forecast 24 unseen samples. We scored the largest recovered
pole modulus, qualitative class (monotone, alternating, or ringing), and
holdout normalized root-mean-square error (NRMSE). The NRMSE is the holdout
RMS prediction error divided by the maximum of the clean-window RMS, holdout
RMS, and $10^{-12}$. ``Forecast success'' means NRMSE below $0.1$. Ground
truth was used only after fitting. Leading-radius error is the absolute
difference between the largest recovered and true normalized pole moduli.

Two independent seeded sweeps were run. Table~\ref{tab:runs} reports launcher
wall time, which slightly overstates billable instance time. At the verified
rate of \$1.428/hour, estimated compute was \$0.782 total. Both instances
terminated and all temporary cloud resources were removed.

\section{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:aws-results} summarizes the two most important empirical
boundaries: information arriving after the hidden lag, and prediction
remaining reliable after modal identification degrades.

\paragraph{Sub-lag observations are blind.}
Table~\ref{tab:horizon} isolates noiseless finite-horizon traces beginning at
the origin. Modal-class accuracy was zero throughout $T/D<1$. This empirical
zero is consistent with Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax}, although the exact result
concerns source recovery rather than a particular modal classifier's error.
There is no rapid recovery immediately after the lag: the generic six-mode
baseline remained poor even for $T/D\ge2$. These schemas can have degree 34
and crowded near-periodic modes, so the post-lag failure is an empirical
limitation of the baseline, not a second impossibility theorem.

\paragraph{The estimator is not universally ineffective.}
Random dyadic-split recurrences were much easier once observations began after
the largest lag. In the noiseless broad run, class accuracy was 78.3\%, 88.9\%,
and 86.6\% for windows 24, 48, and 96; forecast success was 95.6\%, 99.5\%, and
99.8\%. The corresponding sampled median leading-radius errors were 0.0024,
0.0007, and 0.0005. Thus the hard-family failure is structurally selective.

\paragraph{Prediction can survive a false explanation.}
For post-lag random-split traces of length 96, raising relative noise from zero
to $10^{-3}$ reduced modal-class accuracy from 86.6\% to 32.1\% and increased
sampled median radius error from 0.0005 to 0.4453. Forecast success nevertheless
remained 99.8\%. A finite-window predictor can therefore extrapolate accurately
while assigning the wrong modal geometry. This is the most practically
important empirical warning: predictive fit, modal identification, and source
identification are three different evidentiary levels.

\section{What the experiment establishes}

The exact results characterize every finite-prefix equivalence class, exhibit
exponentially many indistinguishable fixed-invariant sources, extend the
obstruction to arbitrary parameter-independent noise, and give a constructive
decoder once a lag bound is known. The experiment then shows where a standard
spectral tool works and where it does not. Massive replication makes the family
contrast stable; it does not turn the classical matrix-pencil algorithm into a
novel method.

Several limitations matter. The schemas are synthetic and one-dimensional;
real program traces may contain input-dependent branching and measurement
artifacts. The modal baseline is capped at order six, while the hardest
recurrences have much larger degree. Root calculations for high-degree,
near-periodic polynomials are themselves conditioned problems. The logarithmic
takeoff profile is only asymptotically a finite exponential sum, and very small
late corrections approach floating-point resolution. Finally, millions of
trials do not remove bias from the chosen schema distribution.

The next positive result should exploit information the baseline ignores:
\[
a_j\in\mathbb Z_{\ge0},\qquad
\sum_j a_j2^{-j}=1,
\]
together with a declared lag bound and sparsity. A constraint-aware decoder can
enumerate or optimize over feasible dyadic recurrences, forward-simulate their
takeoff curves, and compare them on held-out samples. The academically useful
test is whether those constraints shift the recovery boundary as a function of
$T/D$, noise, pole separation, and modal radius. Real recursive traces should
then test whether the recovered equivalence classes remain stable outside the
synthetic model.

\section{Conclusion}

The mathematical boundary is now explicit. A finite trace discloses its total
branching and all coefficients below the observation horizon---no more and no
less. Beyond that horizon, exponentially many fixed-speed, aperiodic sources
can remain observationally identical; adding parameter-independent noise or a
finite pre-return holdout cannot manufacture the missing information. On the
positive side, a declared lag bound and the $D+1$ exact samples through $F_D$
recover the entire recurrence by triangular inversion. Separately, common
terminal speed does not control transient duration: near-periodic kernels can
settle arbitrarily slowly.

The computation reinforces rather than replaces those results. Across 1.16
million hidden schemas and 32.67 million blinded trials, a classical
matrix-pencil estimator performed well on many visible short-lag responses but
failed selectively on the constructed hidden-lag family. With relative noise
$10^{-3}$, 24-step forecast success remained 99.8\% while modal-class accuracy
fell to 32.1\%. The central lesson is therefore a hierarchy of evidence:
forecasting a response, identifying its modes, and recovering its recursive
source are progressively stronger achievements. Claims about mechanism should
stop exactly where the available horizon and structural assumptions stop.
